Alan Turing

Alan Mathison Turing was a British mathematician who had a great influence on mathematics, computer science, cryptanalysis, logic, philosophy, and theoretical biology. He is known to be the father of theoretical computer science. His papers are known as the foundation of Artificial Intelligence. His work on breaking the Enigma code used by Nazis for radio communications was crucial to the Allies and ending the war.

Timeline

1912: Birth in London
1931-34: Undergraduate at King's College, University of Cambridge
1935: Elected fellow of King's College, University of Cambridge for his dissertation "Central Limit Theorem of Probability"
1936: Outlining the Turing Machine in "On Computable Numbers, with an Application to the Entscheidungsproblem"
1936-38: Princeton University. Ph.D.
1939-45: Working on the decryption of the German Enigma cipher machine in WW2
1945-46: National Physical Laboratory, London, Working on developing an electronic digital stored-program computing machine
1946: Published the first detailed design of a stored-program computer
1947: Produced a seminal work on Intelligent Machinery
1048-49: Working on software for earliest stored-program computers
1950: Introducing a test for machine intelligence later known as the Turing Test
1951: Elected as a fellow of the Royal Society
1952-54: Working on mathematical biology
1954: Death (suicide) by cyanide poisoning, Wilmslow, Cheshire
